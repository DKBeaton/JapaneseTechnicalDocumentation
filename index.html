Nouns and Pronouns

Nouns can be one of the easiest words to learn in Japanese. However, memorizing over 6,000 words isn't an easy task. When you learn nouns, you create a framework that is paired with grammar to express the things we want to talk about.

We will also introduce pronouns, which are words that indirectly refer to people, direction, and things that require context to be properly understood.

Nouns

In simple terms, a noun 名詞めいし represents a person, place, state, quality, event, or thing. In Japanese, nouns have no number or gender. This means that there is no distinction between singular and plural forms or masculine and feminine forms. There is also no articles like "a", "an", or "the" that accompany nouns like in English.

The following table shows the most common nouns in Japanese. These words are written with the most basic Kanji漢字かんじ that are taught early on in Japanese education.

Grammar Note: Making nouns plural, although not common, is still possible. One method involves the suffixたち, which is typically used to refer to a group of people or (living) things.

Examples:

1. 女性じょせいたち

(Group of) women.



2. 男性だんせいたち

(Group of) men.



3. 犬いぬたち

(Group of) dogs.

Proper Nouns

In English, a proper noun is a noun that indicates an individual person, place, organization, etc. and is spelled with an initial capital letter. In Japanese, words are not capitalized, but the concept of a proper noun 固有名詞こゆうめいし still exists.

Loanwords

A loanword is a word borrowed from another lanuage. Loanwords in Japanese are called 外来語がいらいご and are typically borrowed from modern languages in the last two to three centuries. Words borrowed from the Chinese language during it's development are called 漢語かんご and are treated separately. Words that came in recent centuries are treated as 外来語がいらいご.

The vast majority of loanwords come from English. We must remember to treat these loanwords as Japanese words. This means you can't simpy pronounce it as if it were English. Some examples can be found in the following table.

Table: Loanwords in Japanese.
Loanwords Meaning

ゲーム Game

パイ Pie/pi
ポイント Point

Grammatical Person

A pronoun 代名詞だいめいし indirectly refers to an entity that involves a person, direction, or thing. The meaning of said entity is determined by context. For instance, proper names are pronouns because they stand in place of the actual person/thing they reference. Proper names can also be shared with others or other things, and so we need context to truly understand what is meant by saying the name "Seth"

Words like "here" and "there" or even words like "this" and "that" are pronouns. This is becasue no one can ascertain what they refer to without context.

Generally when we think of pronouns, we think of pronouns that are used to establish grammatical person. The following tables show the distinctions in English of grammatical person.

Table: Grammatical person in English.
Person Singular Plural
1st I We
2nd You You (all)
3rd He/she/it They

Gender and number both play a role in English in determining what grammatical person is used in a sentence. However, in Japanese there isn't a single pronoun that corresponds to each of the pronouns for grammatical person. This means there is more than one word for "I", "we", etc. This is beacuse all pronouns in Japanese started out as typical nouns, or were far vaguer pronouns that didn't match up with grammatical person.

In Japanese, pronouns differ by their politeness and by who actually uses them. Many pronouns are reserved for whether the speaker is male or female, or whether the person is young or old. Dialects also effect which pronouns are used.

The following pronouns listed in the table below are the most essential.

1st 私わたし
僕ぼく		 私わたしたち

2nd あなた あなたたち
3rd 彼かれ

Usage Notes:

  1. わたくし is the respectful form of 私わたし. Typically わたし will suffice in most situtations. However, "I" is often dropped altogether. As long as it's known that the sentence is about oneself, there is no need to use the pronoun "I".
  2. 僕ぼく is another pronoun for "I" and is used heavily by male speakers, both young and old.
  3. There are many pronouns for "you", but the most neutral in terms of politeness and purpose is あなた. However it is also typically dropped from most sentences. It is inherently direct, which is a quality not typically found in statments directed at others. Second person statments are rarely stated in absolute terms, usually referring to others in third person.
  4. あなた may be used as a term of endearment to refer to one's male partner/spouse, both when one is happy and mad at one's significant other.
  5. In casual settings, the pronouns 彼かれ and 彼女かのじょ may respectively mean "boyfriend" and "girlfriend".
  6. The plural form of 彼かれ, 彼かれら uses the ら suffix making it plural.
  7. 彼かれら may refer to a group of people with both men and women, but 彼女かのじょたち only refers to groups of women.
  8. The pronoun "it" is omitted because it's combined in Japanese with the concept of "that".

In English, pronouns change form depending on their grammatical purpose in a sentence. In grammar, this is called grammatical case. Pronouns change the most in English depending on their case. An example is "my" which is the possessive form of "I".

In Japanese, grammatical case is marked by the use of "case particles". Case particles take the place of form change to noun/pronouns to indicate what function the word has in the sentence. For pronouns, there are two grammatical cases you should know about: nominative and possessive.

  • Nominative Case (subjective): Noun/pronoun used as the subject of the sentence (person or thing that is doing an action).
  • Possessive Case: Noun/pronoun form that shows ownership of one noun by another.
  1. I am an America man. (I = nominative)
  2. Your dog is adorable. (Your = possessive)
  3. We are scientists. (We = nominative)
  4. Our goal is world peace. (Our = possessive)

The subject of a sentence is the person/thing that performs an action or exhibits some description which is what the sentence is about. By adding a case particle to a noun/pronoun we express these grammatical concepts. We add が to the noun/pronoun for nominative cases. We use の for possessive cases. Place and Things

Pronouns used for place and thing are also not used exactly like their English counterparts. See the following table for examples.

Table: Places and things in Japanese.
Close to speaker Close to listener/known only to speaker Far from speaker and listener/known to both speaker and listener
Here There Over there
ここ そこ あそこ
This That That over there
これ それ あれ

Table notes: これ is the closest Japanese equivalent to "it".

When speaking about entities physically visible, there is a three-way distinction made based on the proximity of the entity from the speaker and listener. When the entity discussed is not physically visible, there is a two-way distinction made based on who knows abut the entity in question. The criterion then becomes whether only the speaker knows about the entity or if both the speaker and listener(s) know about it.
